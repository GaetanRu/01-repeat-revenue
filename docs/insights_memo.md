# Insights Memo — Meridian Home Goods Customer Retention Analysis
**Period:** January–December 2011  
**Prepared for:** CFO (Sarah Okafor), CMO (James Reinholt)  
**Analyst:** [Your Name]

---

## Executive Summary

Meridian Home Goods revenue is dangerously concentrated in a small repeat-buyer base. The top 10% of customers generate 60% of all revenue. Over £1.1M in annual revenue is at risk from lapsing high-value customers who have not purchased in 4–8 months. Targeted retention investment in two segments — At-Risk and Cannot Lose — represents the highest-ROI action available to the business.

---

## Q1: What % of revenue comes from repeat vs. one-time buyers?

| Buyer Type | Customers | Revenue | Share |
|------------|-----------|---------|-------|
| Repeat Buyers | 2,708 (64%) | £7,467,894 | **93.1%** |
| One-Time Buyers | 1,510 (36%) | £551,773 | **6.9%** |

**Finding:** 36% of the customer base contributes only 7% of revenue. Acquisition of one-time buyers delivers minimal return. Retention of repeat buyers is the primary revenue lever.

## Q3: What is the cohort retention rate at Month 1, 3, and 6?

| Month | Avg Retention Rate | Industry Benchmark |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Month 1 | 21.0% | 20–30% |
| Month 3 | 24.7% | — |
| Month 6 | ~26.1% | — |

**Finding:** Month 1 retention of 21% sits at the floor of the industry benchmark. Notably, retention increases through Month 3–6, indicating a B2B/wholesale-heavy customer base with quarterly buying cycles rather than monthly impulse purchases. Customers who return after Month 1 tend to stay — the drop-off problem is concentrated in the first 30 days.

## Q4: How concentrated is revenue? (Pareto)

| Customer Tier | Customers | Revenue Share | Cumulative |
|--------------|-----------|--------------|------------|
| Top 10% | 422 | 60.0% | 60.0% |
| Top 20% | 844 | 13.6% | 73.5% |
| Top 30% | 1,266 | 8.5% | 82.0% |
| Bottom 50% | 2,110 | 8.2% | — |

**Finding:** Revenue follows a 60/10 rule — more extreme than the classic 80/20. Losing a single top-decile customer is equivalent to losing 120 bottom-decile customers. Churn prevention must be ruthlessly prioritized for the top tier.

---

## Q5: How much annual revenue is at risk from lapsing customers?

| Segment | Customers | Revenue at Risk | Avg Days Inactive | Priority |
|---------|-----------|----------------|-------------------|----------|
| At-Risk | 658 | £817,189 | 136 days | High |
| Cannot Lose | 225 | £217,578 | 173 days | Urgent |
| Lost | 543 | £120,085 | 251 days | Low |
| **Total** | **1,426** | **£1,154,852** | — | — |

**Finding:** £1.15M in revenue is at risk. At-Risk customers (136 days inactive) are still recoverable — a 30% win-back rate would recover ~£245K. Cannot Lose customers are approaching the point of no return at 173 days.

---

## Recommendations

1. **Launch an At-Risk win-back campaign immediately** — 658 customers, £817K at stake, avg £1,242 per customer. Personalised outreach (email + offer) targeting customers with 90–150 days of inactivity. Even 25% recovery = ~£204K.

2. **Protect Champions with a VIP programme** — 1,072 customers generating £5.35M. Any churn here has outsized revenue impact. Priority access, account management, or loyalty perks at minimal cost.

3. **Accelerate Month-1 conversion for New/Promising** — 322 customers with avg 18-day recency and only 1.2 orders. A targeted second-purchase incentive within 30 days of first order could lift Month-1 retention from 21% toward 26%.

4. **Deprioritise Lost segment outreach** — 543 customers averaging only £221 revenue each, 251 days inactive. ROI on win-back is low. Reallocate budget to At-Risk.
